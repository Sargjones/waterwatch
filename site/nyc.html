Infrastructure Context — Delaware Aqueduct

The world's longest tunnel is leaking — and the fix requires cutting NYC's water in half

The Delaware Aqueduct is a 137-mile continuous concrete pressure tunnel bored through bedrock between 1939 and 1944 — at the time, the largest public works project in the world. It carries approximately 50% of New York City's daily water supply from the Rondout Reservoir in the Catskills to the Kensico Reservoir in Westchester. For over 80 years, it has operated without interruption. It is now leaking.

1937–1945
Delaware Aqueduct bored and completed. A 13.5-foot diameter tunnel through 85 miles of Hudson Valley and Westchester bedrock — the largest continuous rock tunnel in the world at the time.
1990s
DEP first identifies leakage at two locations: near Roseton (Hudson River crossing, Ulster County) and Wawarsing. Leaks estimated at 15–35 million gallons per day.
2010
DEP begins planning a bypass tunnel — a new parallel segment around the two leak zones, avoiding a full shutdown of the main aqueduct.
2013–2021
Bypass tunnel construction. The 2.5-mile bypass at Roseton and the 1-mile bypass at Wawarsing are bored through bedrock in parallel to the live aqueduct.
2026 — Active
Delaware Aqueduct planned shutdown for bypass connection. NYC must operate on Catskill system + Croton system alone (~50% normal supply) during the repair window. Full reservoir storage (99%+) and conservation protocols are critical to this operation.
Post-repair
Estimated 35 million gallons/day of leak eliminated. Delaware Aqueduct expected to operate for another 50+ years without major intervention.

The reservoir system's current near-capacity status (98.9%) is not a coincidence — DEP has been managing storage levels in anticipation of the shutdown, when the city will depend entirely on the Catskill Aqueduct and Croton system. The Esopus Creek (01362500) and West Branch Delaware (01417500) flow readings are therefore operationally critical during this period.

No filter. 9.5 million people. The most consequential water science decision in American history.

The Catskill/Delaware system — which provides 88–90% of New York City's drinking water — is not filtered. Water flows from the Catskill Mountains through 137 miles of tunnel directly to UV treatment and chloramine disinfection, then into the taps of 9.5 million people. This is only possible because of the Filtration Avoidance Determination (FAD), a regulatory status granted by the New York State Department of Health that recognises the Catskill/Delaware watershed is clean enough that filtration is unnecessary.

The FAD is renewed approximately every 10 years and requires NYC to meet strict watershed protection commitments: limits on development, agricultural practices, and septic systems across a 2,000-square-mile area; monitoring at hundreds of stations; rapid response to contamination events. The estimated cost of building a filtration plant for the Catskill/Delaware system — should the FAD ever be lost — is conservatively estimated at $10–15 billion, plus hundreds of millions annually to operate.

Only two large US cities hold Filtration Avoidance Determinations: New York City and Seattle. For NYC, it represents the most consequential water science decision in American municipal history — and one of the most successful examples of watershed protection as infrastructure investment anywhere in the world.

The other side of the water cycle: 700 outfalls, 1.3 billion gallons a day, and a harbour that came back from the dead

Approximately 60% of New York City is served by a combined sewer system, where a single pipe carries both wastewater and stormwater runoff. On a dry day, this all flows to one of NYC's 14 Wastewater Resource Recovery Facilities (WRRFs), which together treat approximately 1.3 billion gallons of wastewater daily before discharging to New York Harbour and the Atlantic Ocean. On a wet day — when rainfall overwhelms the system — combined sewer overflows (CSOs) discharge a mixture of stormwater and untreated sewage directly to the harbour through over 700 outfall pipes.

This is the part of the water cycle that connects conservation to the ocean. When New Yorkers use less water, sewage flows drop, reducing wet-weather overflow risk. When they overuse or when pavement prevents infiltration, the overflow burden grows. NYC has reduced CSO volumes by more than 80% since 1986 through billions in plant upgrades, real-time sewer controls, and green infrastructure. New York Harbour is measurably cleaner than at any point in the past century — striped bass, blue crabs, and bottlenose dolphins now inhabit waters that were effectively dead in the 1960s.

NYC DEP samples 85 stations across the harbour. Wastewater plant performance data (monthly, all 14 facilities) and harbour water quality data are publicly available via NYC Open Data.
