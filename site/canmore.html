River Road & low-lying areas: groundwater flooding risk. The downtown aquifer responds to both Bow River levels and sustained rainfall. When the river runs high — especially combined with heavy rain — groundwater pressure rises and can enter basements even when the Bow has not overflowed its banks. Sustained Bow at Banff readings above 150 cms signal elevated risk. This is distinct from overbank flooding and is not captured by standard flood advisories.
Rundle Forebay levels are not publicly available — EPCOR operates on SCADA. Spray River at Banff is the best available public proxy.

Year-round conservation — Canmore

High impact
Watch the Bow at Banff in July–August
Once snowmelt ends the Bow drops sharply. Low Banff discharge is the earliest public signal of potential restrictions — before EPCOR or the Town announce anything. This dashboard will show it first.
High impact
River Road residents: check Banff after rain
When Bow at Banff exceeds 150 cms — especially after sustained rain — check your basement. The shallow aquifer responds within hours. Sump pumps should be tested each spring.
High impact
Don't swim in the Rundle Forebay
The Rundle Forebay supplies roughly half of Canmore's drinking water. Recreational use increases treatment costs and contamination risk.
Medium impact
Water gardens before 7am
Canmore's dry mountain air means higher evaporation rates than coastal cities. Early morning watering loses 30% less water than midday.
Medium impact
Report leaks to EPCOR: 403.609.6400
Canmore's aging distribution system has historically lost 20–25% of treated water to leaks. Every reported leak matters at that scale.
Medium impact
Sign up for Voyent Alert
The Town uses Voyent Alert for restriction notifications. Free at canmore.ca — you'll know about restrictions before your neighbour does.
